\title{Thick Reality Front – exploratory numerical framework}

\author{Saku H\"am\"al\"ainen}

\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}

We introduce a computational framework for exploring a
``thick reality front'' description of quantum dynamics.
Instead of associating physical reality with an instantaneous
wavefunction at a single time, the model assumes that observable
structure emerges from a finite temporal region surrounding the
present. The framework combines forward quantum evolution with
backward propagation conditioned on detection events, producing
an effective density field that depends on both preparation and
measurement. Numerical simulations of a double-slit configuration
demonstrate the emergence of coherent ridge trajectories in the
probability density evolution. The approach is compatible with
standard quantum predictions while offering a geometric
interpretation of probability flow in spacetime.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Quantum mechanics describes the evolution of a system through the
Schr\"odinger equation

\begin{equation}
i\hbar \partial_t \psi = H\psi .
\end{equation}

While the equation predicts the evolution of the wavefunction,
measurement outcomes appear to select definite results that are
not described by unitary evolution alone.

Several interpretations address this issue, including collapse
models, Bohmian mechanics, and time-symmetric formulations of
quantum mechanics. In this work we explore a computational
framework inspired by time-symmetric ideas but formulated as a
numerical simulation method.

The key concept is that observable structure may not correspond
to a single time slice of the wavefunction. Instead, the effective
``reality'' may emerge from a finite temporal region surrounding
the present. We refer to this region as a \textit{thick reality
front}.

\section{Thick Reality Front Formalism}

\subsection{Forward evolution}

Consider a system prepared at time $t_0$ with initial state
$\psi_0$. Forward evolution is given by

\begin{equation}
\psi_f(t) = U(t,t_0)\psi_0 .
\end{equation}

For Schr\"odinger dynamics the wavefunction satisfies

\begin{equation}
i\hbar \partial_t \psi_f(x,t) = H\psi_f(x,t).
\end{equation}

The forward probability density is

\begin{equation}
\rho_f(x,t) = |\psi_f(x,t)|^2 .
\end{equation}

\subsection{Backward propagation}

Suppose a detection occurs at position $x_d$ and time $t_d$.
We define a localized detection state

\begin{equation}
\phi(x,t_d) =
\exp\left(
-\frac{(x-x_d)^2}{2\sigma_c^2}
\right).
\end{equation}

Backward propagation is defined through

\begin{equation}
\phi(t) = U^\dagger(t_d,t)\phi(t_d).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Temporal thickness}

Instead of associating reality with a single detection time,
we consider a temporal window around $t_d$. Let $t_k$ denote
candidate detection times within a region of width $\sigma_T$.

The temporal weights are

\begin{equation}
w_k =
\frac{
\exp[-(t_k-t_d)^2/(2\sigma_T^2)]
}{
\sum_j
\exp[-(t_j-t_d)^2/(2\sigma_T^2)]
}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Retrodictive field}

Backward states generate a retrodictive weighting field

\begin{equation}
E_{\mathrm{mix}}(x,t)
=
\sum_k
w_k
|\phi_k(x,t)|^2 .
\end{equation}

\subsection{Thick reality front density}

Observable structure emerges from the product of forward
probability and retrodictive weighting.

\begin{equation}
\Gamma(x,t) =
\rho_f(x,t) E_{\mathrm{mix}}(x,t).
\end{equation}

To obtain a normalized distribution we define

\begin{equation}
\rho_{\mathrm{TRF}}(x,t)
=
\frac{
\rho_f(x,t) E_{\mathrm{mix}}(x,t)
}{
\int dx \,
\rho_f(x,t) E_{\mathrm{mix}}(x,t)
}.
\end{equation}

In two dimensions

\begin{equation}
\rho_{\mathrm{TRF}}(x,y,t)
=
\frac{
|\psi_f(x,y,t)|^2
E_{\mathrm{mix}}(x,y,t)
}{
\iint dx\,dy\,
|\psi_f(x,y,t)|^2
E_{\mathrm{mix}}(x,y,t)
}.
\end{equation}

\section{Ridge Trajectories}

The density $\rho_{\mathrm{TRF}}$ often develops narrow
high-probability structures. These can be tracked through
ridge trajectories defined by

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{r}_R(t)
=
\operatorname*{arg\,max}_{\mathbf{r}}
\rho_{\mathrm{TRF}}(\mathbf{r},t).
\end{equation}

These curves provide a geometric description of probability
flow through the thick reality front.

\section{Velocity Fields and Bohmian Comparison}

The quantum probability current is

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{j} =
\frac{\hbar}{m}
\mathrm{Im}
(\psi^* \nabla \psi).
\end{equation}

This defines a velocity field

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{v} = \frac{\mathbf{j}}{\rho_f}.
\end{equation}

In Bohmian mechanics these velocities define particle
trajectories. In the present framework we compare these
trajectories with ridge trajectories extracted from
$\rho_{\mathrm{TRF}}$.

\section{Connection to the Born Rule}

The thick reality front formalism does not modify the
underlying quantum dynamics. Forward evolution remains
governed by the Schr\"odinger equation.

Detection probabilities are determined by the forward
density

\begin{equation}
P(x_d,t_d) \propto |\psi_f(x_d,t_d)|^2 ,
\end{equation}

which is precisely the Born rule.

Backward propagation only redistributes weight inside the
thick reality front but does not change the detection
probability itself.

Integrating the thick-front density over intermediate
times yields

\begin{equation}
\int \rho_{\mathrm{TRF}}(x,t) \, dx
=
1 ,
\end{equation}

and the marginal detection distribution remains

\begin{equation}
P(x_d) =
|\psi_f(x_d,t_d)|^2 .
\end{equation}

Thus the framework is consistent with the standard Born
probability rule.

\section{Numerical Experiment}

We simulate a two-dimensional double-slit experiment using
a split-operator Fourier method. A Gaussian wave packet
propagates toward a barrier containing two narrow slits.

The evolution sequence is

\begin{verbatim}
wave packet -> double slit -> interference -> screen
\end{verbatim}

Forward evolution produces the interference pattern while
backward propagation generates the retrodictive field.

\section{Relativistic Extension}

The framework can be extended to relativistic dynamics.
For the Dirac equation

\begin{equation}
i\hbar \partial_t \psi =
(c\bm{\alpha}\cdot\mathbf{p}
+ \beta mc^2)\psi ,
\end{equation}

the conserved current is

\begin{equation}
j^\mu = \bar{\psi}\gamma^\mu\psi .
\end{equation}

The velocity field becomes

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{v} =
\frac{\mathbf{j}}{j^0}.
\end{equation}

The thick reality front construction can therefore be
applied to relativistic wave dynamics.

\section{Conclusion}

We introduced a computational framework for exploring
thick reality front models of quantum dynamics.

By combining forward evolution with backward propagation
from detection events, the method produces structured
probability flows that can be analyzed geometrically
through ridge trajectories.

The framework reproduces standard quantum predictions
while providing a new spacetime interpretation of
probability flow and measurement.
